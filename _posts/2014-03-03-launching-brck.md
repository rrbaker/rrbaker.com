## Or, why I hate versioning generalizations

I'm so happy for [Ushahidi's](http://ushahidi.com) [BRCK team](http://brck.com) today as they finally introduce BRCK to the world after nearly a year's worth of experiments, fried circuit boards,

Seeing the excitement and support on Twitter has been great. Thank you to everyone who's retweeted our announcements, [blogged]() [about]() [it](), [published]() [articles]() [about] [it], and, of course, [supported the Kickstarter.](https://www.kickstarter.com/projects/1776324009/brck-your-backup-generator-for-the-internet)

But with this excitement has come doubt, which, I'll say, I'm genuinely, equally excited to see.  "Why is this any better than tethering my phone?" "What happens if a large download flips over to the SIM and eats up my data?" "How will you seamlessly flip from WiFi bridge to GSM?"

Some of these have easy answers. On the phone: Because you can have 20 people on the same network as opposed to the usual three to four and for much longer (around eight hours).

To the other questions: Yeah, we don't know. Yet.

Which is the point.

With all due respect to my current colleagues, consider the Ushahidi platform. Did it and the company achieve their brand by building the greatest crowdsourcing software in the world? No. But our community -- the thousands of individuals and organizations around the world -- used it to create some of the greatest crowdsourcing projects to date.

Open source is people, not code.

We believe it is a v2 of the modems many of us (older folks) grew up with. But it's not a v2 of what's in this space now. It's our addition to the landscape, an amazing landscape forged by teams like [Village Telco], [Commotion], and [Tethr]. No doubt Ushahidi has much to learn from these people and I personally look forward to having more and better conversations with all of them.

And where the community is involved, the best is yet to come.
