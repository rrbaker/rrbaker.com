When Patrick Meier contacted me about getting involved with Somalia Speaks, I reluctantly agreed to join.

Working with Patrick has always been a pleasure, provides a challenge (technically, not personally), and ultimately pushes the envelope. You only need to follow [his blog] to see how true that is.

But here's the goal: track the worst areas and people migrations using crowdsourced information during the height of the drought, which was already well underway.

Never happen.

Admittedly I've said yes to enough projects built on mobile phones and good intentions. A lot of effort for almost no impact. This seemed no different.

- No time to develop trust networks around the country
- No time to work with the many NGOs on the ground in the country working to combat the drought
- Most crowdsourcing campaigns feature one-and-done submissions from the public. Follow-up is extremely hard and, when it does work, linking reports is equally hard, manual, and time-consuming. And we're going to track migration patterns?
-

But we did it anyway. And it's working. And we have the Somali diaspora to thank for that.

Here's the difference: instead of trying to start from government channels, telco channels,

We pay the diaspora for their service.
